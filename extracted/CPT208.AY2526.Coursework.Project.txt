Instructions for the Process Portfolio
In this submission, you should present your work with a focus on the followings:
1. Demonstrate an iterative design, prototyping, and evaluation process
2. Explain the prototypes used in the design process, with comparisons of alternatives
3. Use appropriate methods to evaluate the design alternatives
4. Show a valid collection and analysis of data from the correct target user
demographics with reasonable sample sizes
5. Demonstrate a good discussion on the evaluation results and design implications
Specifically, the Process Portfolio should be a window to showcase your work. You should
cover the following content in your portfolio:
1. Motivation & Research
• The Why: A 200-word statement on why your group chose your specific track
(Heritage, Social, or Active).
• The Gap: Summarize 4 academic papers and 4 commercial products. Instead of a
summary, list 3 things they did well and 3 things they missed.
• The Stakeholders: Define primary and secondary users with distinct Personas.
2. User Requirements
• User Journey Map: A visual map showing the current pain points of your target
users.
• Requirements List: A bulleted list of 3 must-haves for the system to be considered
“Playful”.
• Evidence of Life: Upload 5 photos or short clip videos of your group interviewing a
potential user or observing the site (e.g., Suzhou Grand Canal).
3. Ideation & Alternatives
• The “Crazy Eights”: Photos of 8 rapid hand-drawn sketches of different UI ideas.
• Design Alternatives: Compare at least 2-3 different ways the system could work and
explain why you chose one over the other(s).
• Low-Fi Prototype: A link to a clickable Figma prototype.
4. Technical Implementation
• System Architecture: A diagram showing how your Web App handles data.
• High-Fi Prototype: Ideally a URL to the hosted system (e.g., yourproject.github.io or
yourproject.vercel.app).
• Individual Contributions: A table showing exactly what each of the 4 members built
(Code, UI Design, Content, or Testing, etc.).
5. Evaluation & Reflection
• Usability Testing: Results from testing your “Alpha” version with 3 real people.
• Iterative Refinement: “Before and After” screenshots showing a change you made
based on user feedback.
• Final Reflection: A brief discussion on the social/ethical implications of your design,
and the use of AI in this project (check more details in the Generative AI Permissions
section).

Instructions for the System Development
The system is the functional heart of your project. It must be a live, interactive web application
that demonstrates your group’s solution to the identified human-centric challenge.
Key Objectives:
1. Functionality: Deliver a working prototype that users can interact with via a URL.
2. User Experience: Demonstrate a high-fidelity interface that aligns with your design
requirements.
3. Technical Deployment: Show proficiency in deploying to modern cloud platforms
(e.g., GitHub pages, Vercel).
4. Accessibility: Ensure the system is usable and follows basic web standards.
Required Content:
1. Live URL: A direct link to your hosted web app. Ensure the site is “Public” and
remains active throughout the marking period.
2. Core Feature Set: The system must implement at least 3 must-have playful features
identified in your Requirements List.
3. Responsive Design: The interface should be optimized for the device specified in
your track (e.g., Mobile / PC).
4. Source Code Repository: A link to your group’s GitHub repository.
o README.md: Must include setup instructions and a list of technologies used
(e.g., Three.js, React).
o Vibe Coding Logs: If AI was used for coding, include a folder named /ai-
logs containing the primary prompts used to generate core components.
5. Data Handling: Evidence of how the system manages user input or interaction states
(as outlined in your Portfolio’s System Architecture diagram).

Instructions for the Poster Submission
In the poster presentation, you should complete the following tasks:
1. Clearly state the project motivation, as well as aims and objectives
2. Clearly specify and study the target group of users
3. Research extensively and summarize the key issues on the existing works
4. Show some efforts in collecting design requirements
5. Propose effective and novel design solutions, use prototypes where possible
Poster Size: A1, 59.5 x 84.1 cm (23.4 x 33.1 inches).
Specifically, you should cover the following content in your poster:
1. Header & Brand
• Project Title: A catchy, playful name for your system (e.g., “Suzhou Whispers” instead
of “Project A1”).
• Team Info: Group ID, member names and student IDs.
2. The Problem Space
• Clearly define the motivation behind your project.
• Briefly highlight what is missing in existing commercial or academic solutions.
• Identify your primary and secondary user groups using a concise Persona snapshot.
3. The Design Solutions
• State your specific aims and objectives.
• Describe your novel design concept.
• Explain what makes this experience engaging or human-centric.
• A simplified visual (e.g., user journey map) showing how a user interacts with your
system to solve their pain points.
4. Prototypes & Iteration
• Show the evolution from early sketches (low-fi) to your current web-based prototype
(high-fi).
• A small diagram showing how the system works (e.g., WebXR on Vercel).
• Explain one major design decision you made between 2-3 options.
5. Early Evaluation & Impact
• Summary of any pilot testing or requirements collected.
• Why does this project matter in the context of the topic or human-centric design?
6. References & Acknowledgments
• AI Disclosure: Include the required citation for any AI-generated images used in the
poster (e.g., Gemini Nano Banana) as per university policy.
• Academic References: List at least 3 key papers that influenced your research.

Instructions for Video Demo Submission
The video is your “pitch” to the world. It should be a concise, engaging 2-minute demonstration
that captures the vibe and impact of your system.
Format: MP4 encoding (with H.264 compression), 1080p.
For more details, check out the two guides: https://sigchi.org/resources/guides-for-
authors/videos/ & https://chi2025.acm.org/guide-to-a-successful-video-submission/
Key Objectives:
1. Narrative: Tell a story that starts with a user's pain point and ends with your playful
solution.
2. Clarity: Use screen recordings to show exactly how the system works.
3. Engagement: Use high-quality audio and visuals to maintain a standard teaser video.
Required Content:
1. Cover: Show a representative image of your project, with your group ID, project title,
your names, and student IDs.
2. The Hook (~10s) Visually demonstrate the problem space (e.g., footage of a crowded
site in Suzhou or a lonely student study session).
3. The Concept (~15s): Introduce your system and its core human-centric value
proposition.
4. The Walkthrough (~55s): High-quality screen recordings of the High-Fi prototype in
action.
o Show a user navigating through the 3 core features.
o Use call-outs (text overlays) to highlight specific playful interactions.
5. The Impact (~35s): Show clips of evaluation results or quote from your usability testing.
6. Closing & Credits (~5s): Acknowledge the help your received throughout the project;
display the mandatory AI Disclosure if AI-generated voices or visuals were used.

University Policy on Late Submissions
If you submit coursework after the deadline, you will be penalized:
• 5% of the total marks available for the assessment will be deducted from the
assessment mark for each working day after the submission deadline, up to a
maximum of 25%;
• Coursework received more than five working days after the submission deadline will

University Policy on Academic Integrity
The University aims to foster a learning environment which produces students who embrace
academic integrity, understand that they must produce their own work, are able to acknowledge
explicitly any material that has been included from other sources or legitimate collaboration,
and to present their own findings, conclusions or data based on appropriate and ethical
practice.
The University will support you to understand the standards of academic integrity, while you
are responsible for learning and upholding professional standards of research, writing,
assessment, and ethics in your area of study. Violation of academic integrity comes in many
forms, including but not limited to the following:
• improper citation or referencing;
• unauthorised collaboration with another person in the preparation and production of a
submitted work;
• copying directly from other persons without their knowledge as your own work;
• submitting all or part of the same academic work for two or more modules without
permission;
• consciously representing another’s work or concept as your own without proper
acknowledgment and citation of the sources;
• altering data obtained by legitimate means or making up a portion or whole set of data
and reporting them in your own assignment;
• requesting another party to prepare all or part of an assignment (with or without
payment) on your behalf.
Any violation of academic integrity is a serious offence and is therefore subject to an appropriate
penalty. According to the individual case and the seriousness of the offence, penalties applied
will vary and may include one or a combination of the following:
• a written warning;
• a mark penalty or a zero mark for the assessment;
• a zero mark for the module;
• a note on student’s records;
• suspension of studies;
• termination of studies.
In addition to the respective penalty imposed, you may also be given feedback on how to avoid
further offence in future work.
